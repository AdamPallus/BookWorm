# Example Wiki Page

This is one page from a hypothetical wiki for *Hyperion* by Dan Simmons. Treat
it as a reference for what a *good* page looks like in practice — the prose
voice, the structure, and especially the link density. The Red Rising wiki
you are building should look like this in feel, not in content.

The hypothetical state: the agent has just finished integrating the segment
that contains "The Priest's Tale" — the first of the seven pilgrims' stories.
Everything Hoyt-related from that tale is on the page; nothing from later
tales or later books is on it.

Note the things this page does:

- The **Summary** is two sentences. It does not just list facts ("priest from
  Pacem"); it tells the reader what the *angle* on this character is —
  pilgrimage, the cruciforms, the constant pain. That angle is what the Q&A
  tagline needs.
- The page is **prose**, not a stat block. There are no bullet lists of
  attributes, no "Family:" headings, no "Affiliation:" lines. A reader could
  read the page top-to-bottom as a coherent essay.
- **Sections fit the subject.** A character whose past is the cause of his
  present situation gets sections in roughly that order: Background, the
  defining catastrophe, the current situation, outlook. A different
  character would want different sections.
- **Every first mention of a known subject is a markdown link.** Look at how
  many there are: Catholic Church, Pacem, Hegemony, Hyperion, Bikura,
  cruciform, Father Paul Duré, the Shrike, the Time Tombs, the Shrike
  Pilgrimage, the Consul, Martin Silenus, Sol Weintraub. Subsequent
  mentions on the same page are plain text. This page would be wrong with
  zero links.
- The page is **substantial** — about 350 words. A central character whose
  story is half the book deserves this much space. A walk-on character
  would not have a page at all.
- It does **not** say "as we'll see" or "later in the book" or "it turns out
  that". It just states what the wiki currently knows, as if this is the
  entry.

# Father Lenar Hoyt

## Summary
A young Jesuit priest of the [Catholic Church](../factions/catholic-church.md), Lenar Hoyt is one of the seven pilgrims chosen to walk to the [Time Tombs](../places/time-tombs.md) and confront the [Shrike](shrike.md). He carries the journals — and the parasitic [cruciforms](../concepts/cruciform.md) — of his predecessor [Father Paul Duré](paul-dure.md), and his pilgrimage is shaped by constant pain and an unwilling immortality.

## Background

Hoyt is from [Pacem](../places/pacem.md), the small backwater world that has become the seat of a contracting Catholic Church under the [Hegemony of Man](../factions/hegemony.md). The Church, once dominant on Old Earth, persists in his era as a marginal institution, and Hoyt belongs to the generation that has known it only in decline. He is intelligent, devout, physically frail by the time the pilgrimage assembles, and visibly in pain.

He was sent to [Hyperion](../places/hyperion.md) early in his career to retrieve the body of Father Paul Duré, an exiled archaeologist whose work among the [Bikura](../concepts/bikura.md) had been silenced by the Church. The mission was meant to be a brief recovery trip. It became the defining catastrophe of his life.

## The Bikura assignment and the cruciforms

In the highland forests of the Cleft on Hyperion, Hoyt encountered the Bikura — a small, isolated tribe of seventy who live in a state of placid, stunted near-immortality. Their condition is sustained by the cruciform, a parasitic cross-shaped organism that fuses to the host's spine and, on death, slowly reconstitutes the body. Each resurrection costs the host some fraction of intelligence, sex, and individuality. The Bikura are what remains after generations of this cycle.

He found Father Duré crucified on a tesla tree above the Cleft, kept alive by his own cruciform, repeatedly killed and resurrected as an act of penance and refusal. Hoyt brought Duré's body and journals back, but in doing so was infected himself — and worse, took on Duré's cruciform as well as his own. He now carries two of the parasites, and the daily agony of resisting their pull is the central fact of his existence.

## The pilgrimage

Hoyt joined the [Shrike Pilgrimage](../events/shrike-pilgrimage.md) at the urging of the Church and out of his own desperation. The pilgrims travel to the Time Tombs in the hope that the Shrike — the legend tells — will grant a single wish to one pilgrim and kill the others. Hoyt's wish, never spoken plainly to the others, is for the cruciforms to be taken from him; whether by death, exorcism, or transformation, he does not specify.

He carries Duré's journals as testimony, and he reads from them when the other pilgrims agree it is his turn to tell his tale. He prays often, sleeps badly, and is the pilgrim most visibly close to collapse. He distrusts the [Consul](consul.md)'s diplomacy and has no patience for [Martin Silenus](martin-silenus.md)'s cynicism, but treats [Sol Weintraub](sol-weintraub.md) with quiet kindness.

## Outlook

Hoyt does not believe the Shrike is divine; he does not believe the cruciform is divine; he does not believe his own suffering carries redemptive meaning. What he has come to believe, increasingly, is that the universe is structured by a cruelty more thorough than any theology accounts for, and that his Church's doctrines have been overrun by a fact older and harder than they were built to name. He continues to pray.
